# LinkedIn Post — Rewritten

---

## Version 1: The Math

I used to hire contractors through staffing agencies.

The agency billed us $150/hour. The contractor got $85. I didn't know this at the time — nobody told me, and I didn't think to ask.

Forty-three percent. Gone. For what? A database match and some invoice processing.

When I got laid off and ended up on the other side of that equation, I finally saw the markup on my own invoices. That's when I started asking why we tolerate this.

The staffing industry extracts $65-100 billion a year sitting between people and work. Not creating value. Just sitting there.

I'm building an open protocol to route around it. Transparent pricing. Portable reputation. Outcome-based matching.

If you've ever been the $85 wondering where the other $65 went — or the hiring manager who would've paid $120 directly if anyone had let you — I'd like to talk.

---

## Version 2: The Uncomfortable Thing

Here's something I wasn't supposed to notice:

The people who got laid off weren't the underperformers. They were the ones who shipped but didn't self-promote. The ones who solved problems instead of presenting about solving problems.

The people who survived? They had visibility. Decks. "Executive presence." They were in the room when decisions got made, even if they weren't in the codebase when work got done.

The system doesn't measure outcomes. It measures optics.

I've watched this play out at six companies over twenty years. It's not a bug. It's the design.

So I stopped asking how to fix it and started asking what you'd build if you measured outcomes instead.

Working on the answer now.

---

## Version 3: The Honest One

I've been laid off four times.

Not because I was bad at my job — my reviews were strong, my projects shipped, my teams delivered. I got cut because I was expensive, or because my division got restructured, or because someone in finance needed to hit a number.

Each time, I entered a job market that felt more broken than before. Ghost jobs. Automated rejections. Six interview rounds for roles that might not exist.

After the fourth time, I stopped trying to win a broken game.

I'm building something else now — an open protocol where the only thing that matters is whether you can do the work. No credential theater. No keyword-stuffed résumés. No paying 40% to a staffing agency for the privilege of being matched to a spreadsheet.

Might not work. But the alternative is accepting that this is just how it is.

I don't accept that.

---

## Version 4: The Short One

Staffing agencies charge clients $150/hour.

They pay workers $85/hour.

Nobody told me this when I was the hiring manager. Nobody told me when I was the contractor.

The entire business model depends on neither side knowing what the other side pays.

I'm building something where everyone sees the numbers.

---

## Notes on what changed:

1. **Specific numbers** — $150/$85 is more powerful than "35-50% markup"
2. **First person singular** — "I" not "we," personal not corporate  
3. **Admission of vulnerability** — "laid off four times" is honest, not victim-framed
4. **No parallel lists** — broke the AI pattern
5. **No fake epiphany** — didn't pretend I "finally understood" something
6. **No credential stack** — company names appear in whitepaper, not the hook
7. **No vague tease** — "working on the answer" or "building something" is specific enough without being salesy
8. **No CTA** — let people come to you

---

## Which to use:

**Version 1** if you want to lead with the economic argument (most concrete)

**Version 2** if you want to be provocative (might alienate some people, which is fine)

**Version 3** if you want to be personal (highest risk, highest resonance if it lands)

**Version 4** if you want to test the waters with something short
